How to Set Up CCTV for Professional Monitoring in India: A Step-by-Step NRI Guide

Getting cameras installed is step one. Getting them to work with a professional monitoring service requires the right hardware, network setup, and configuration choices. This guide covers everything, start to finish.

Most NRIs who have cameras at their India property set them up for self-monitoring via a phone app. That's a reasonable start — but if you want to connect to a professional monitoring service, the requirements are different and more specific.

This guide walks you through everything you need to do to get a CCTV system in India ready for 24/7 professional monitoring. It assumes you may already have some cameras and are upgrading, or that you're starting fresh.

What Professional Monitoring Requires

Before getting into steps, it's useful to understand what a professional monitoring service actually needs from your camera system. There are four non-negotiable requirements:

  1. RTSP-compatible cameras — The monitoring system needs to access live streams directly. RTSP (Real-Time Streaming Protocol) is the standard. Cameras that only work via a proprietary mobile app cannot be monitored professionally.
  2. Stable internet connection — The monitoring system accesses your camera streams remotely. Your internet needs to be stable and fast enough to stream at minimum 2–4 cameras simultaneously (at monitoring-quality resolution).
  3. Secure remote access — The monitoring service needs a way to access your streams without exposing your cameras to the public internet. This is typically handled via a VPN (most providers use WireGuard).
  4. Power backup (UPS) — If your cameras and networking equipment go offline during a power cut, there's nothing to monitor. A UPS keeps the system running during outages.
Why does RTSP matter? Many budget cameras sold in India (especially branded for home use) only stream via a manufacturer's cloud app — Ezviz, Reolink app, TP-Link Tapo, and others work this way. If the cloud service goes down, or the manufacturer changes their API, your stream breaks. Professional-grade cameras with RTSP stream directly, reliably, and indefinitely.

What resolution to choose

For professional monitoring, 2MP (1080p) is the minimum. At this resolution, an operator can clearly identify a person at a gate or entrance. 4MP is better for wider coverage areas. 8MP (4K) is overkill for monitoring purposes and uses significantly more bandwidth and storage.

Fixed lens vs. varifocal

Fixed lens cameras (2.8mm or 3.6mm) cover a wide angle and are suitable for most entry points and perimeter views. Varifocal cameras let you adjust the zoom — useful for long driveways or monitoring specific approach paths at a distance. For a typical 4–8 camera residential setup, fixed 2.8mm–4mm lenses cover most needs.

Step 2: Planning Camera Placement

2

Cover the right areas before worrying about coverage area

More cameras don't automatically mean better security. Strategic placement beats volume.

For a standard residential property in India, here's a priority order:

  • Camera 1 — Main gate / entry (essential): Positioned to capture faces and vehicles approaching and at the gate. This is your primary identification camera. Mount at 3–3.5m height facing toward the approach. Coverage: 5–8 metres out from the gate.
  • Camera 2 — Front entrance / main door (essential): Covers the door approach and veranda. Height: 2.5–3m. Angled down to capture the area in front of the door.
  • Camera 3 — Rear/side access (essential if applicable): If there's a second access point — a side lane, back gate, servants' entrance — it needs coverage. Umonitored secondary entry points are where most of intrusions at camera-equipped properties occur.
  • Camera 4 — Property overview (high elevation, wide angle): If the property has a second floor or elevated position, a wide-angle overview camera gives context for what's happening across the property. Mount as high as possible.
  • Camera 5+ — Parking, garden, garage: Priority 2 areas once essentials are covered. Useful for monitoring caretaker activity and vehicle identification.
⚠ Common mistake: covering interior rooms before securing the perimeter. Cameras inside bedrooms or living areas give you views of a property after someone has already entered. Entry point cameras stop problems before they happen — or give you documentation of exactly how and when someone entered. Always start with exterior entry coverage.

Step 3: Internet and Power Requirements

3

Get fibre internet and a UPS — both are non-negotiable

Internet: what you need

  • Type: Fibre broadband (Airtel Xstream, Jio Fiber, BSNL FTTH) — NOT mobile hotspot (4G/5G) and NOT old ADSL/VDSL copper connections
  • Speed: Minimum 10 Mbps upload for a 4-camera setup at monitoring quality (2MP). 20+ Mbps upload for 8+ cameras.
  • Stability: The connection must be stable 24/7. Occasional mobile data as backup is acceptable, but primary monitoring on mobile data is not.
  • Static/consistent IP: Your router's WAN IP will change periodically on most residential connections. A DDNS (Dynamic DNS) service — most good routers support this for free — solves this.

Power: UPS is essential

India's power grid, particularly in residential and semi-urban areas, experiences cuts that range from minutes to hours. When your cameras and router lose power, they go offline — and your property is unmonitored for exactly that window.

A UPS covering your camera NVR (Network Video Recorder) and your networking equipment (router and any switches) is essential. Sizing guide:

  • Minimum: 1000VA UPS providing 4+ hours of runtime for NVR + router + up to 8 cameras
  • Recommended: 1500–2000VA for longer runtime or more cameras
  • Also consider: Solar UPS/inverter setups are increasingly common and cost-effective in India for extended backup coverage

Step 4: NVR and Network Setup

4

Set up a Network Video Recorder and stable local network

An NVR (Network Video Recorder) is the central hub for your camera system. It records footage locally, manages camera streams, and provides the central point through which the monitoring service accesses your cameras.

NVR vs. DVR

DVRs are older technology for analog cameras connected via coax cable. NVRs work with IP cameras connected via Ethernet or WiFi. If you're setting up a new system or using any modern IP cameras, get an NVR. Hikvision, Dahua, and CP Plus all make compatible NVRs.

PoE (Power over Ethernet)

PoE NVRs power your cameras through the same Ethernet cable that carries the video signal — no separate power cable needed per camera. For a fixed installation, this simplifies wiring significantly. A PoE NVR with 4–8 PoE ports is the most practical choice for most residential setups.

Local network setup

  • All cameras, NVR, and the router should be on the same local network
  • Assign static IP addresses to each camera (configured in the camera's settings) so they don't change after router reboots
  • Use quality Cat6 cable for camera connections — not Cat5e, and definitely not WiFi for cameras if you can avoid it
WiFi cameras in India: WiFi cameras are convenient but less reliable than wired in Indian settings — thick walls, interference, and intermittent connection drops are more common. For monitoring reliability, wired Ethernet cameras are strongly preferred. If you must use WiFi for some cameras, use 5GHz WiFi and ensure solid signal strength.

Step 5: Configuring RTSP Streams

5

Enable RTSP on your cameras and note the stream URLs

RTSP streams are how the monitoring system accesses your cameras. Most professional IP cameras have RTSP enabled by default but you should verify and note the stream URLs.

Standard RTSP URL formats

  • Hikvision: rtsp://[user]:[pass]@[camera-ip]:554/Streaming/Channels/101 (main stream) or /102 (sub stream)
  • Dahua: rtsp://[user]:[pass]@[camera-ip]:554/cam/realmonitor?channel=1&subtype=0
  • CP Plus: rtsp://[user]:[pass]@[camera-ip]:554/stream1

You'll use the sub-stream (lower resolution, lower bandwidth) for the monitoring feed — typically 720p. The main stream (full resolution) is for local recording. This keeps bandwidth usage manageable without losing identification quality.

Set strong passwords

Before sharing camera access with a monitoring service, ensure all cameras have strong, unique passwords. Never use default passwords (admin/admin, admin/12345 etc.) — cameras with default credentials are frequently found and accessed by automated scanners on the internet.

Step 6: Connecting to a Monitoring Service

6

Provide your camera details and set up secure remote access

Once your cameras are set up and RTSP is configured, the monitoring service handles the rest of the connection process. Here's how SecurifyHQ does it:

  1. VPN provisioning — We provide you with a WireGuard VPN configuration file. Your router installs this (most modern routers support WireGuard). This creates an encrypted tunnel from your property's network to our monitoring infrastructure — without exposing your cameras to the public internet.
  2. Camera registration — We register each camera in our monitoring platform with its name, location, RTSP URL, and your alert preferences.
  3. Test and verify — Before going live, we test each stream, verify night vision quality, and confirm alert detection is working correctly.
  4. Go live — Monitoring begins. You receive confirmation and can see your camera status through your client portal.
Why WireGuard VPN? Directly forwarding camera ports through your router (port forwarding) is a significant security risk — it exposes your cameras to the public internet where automated attacks are constant. WireGuard creates a secure, private tunnel that only the monitoring service can access. Your cameras are never publicly accessible.

Setup Checklist for Professional Monitoring

Use this before requesting connection to a monitoring service:

  • All cameras are Hikvision, Dahua, CP Plus, or equivalent RTSP-compatible brand
  • All cameras have unique strong passwords (not factory default)
  • Main gate, front entrance, and rear access point all have camera coverage
  • Cameras have good night vision (30m IR range minimum)
  • All cameras connected via wired Ethernet (or stable 5GHz WiFi as last resort)
  • NVR installed and recording locally
  • All cameras assigned static local IP addresses
  • RTSP sub-stream URL confirmed working for each camera
  • Internet connection is fibre broadband (not mobile data)
  • Upload speed tested at ≥10 Mbps
  • UPS installed covering NVR and router
  • Router supports WireGuard VPN (most modern routers do)
